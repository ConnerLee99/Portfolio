The purpose of it in modern business

IT is at the centre of any good business. Whether the business is large or tiny they all rely on a trained IT team. From communication in the business itself between coworkers and mangers to leasing with clients to create what they need. Without a infrastructure to do this, many business would would not be able to function at all. And when these systems go down. It is a team that knows the ins and outs of the system that are there to fix it and keep the business operating. Unless your business is run like its the 60s and you have cabinet upon cabinet full of files. Your data storage is likely to be in the cloud or dedicated servers. It is the job of the IT team to keep this service not only operational but safe and secure. It is all good having a system that works but if it can easily be breached and your data accessed and stolen then the whole system is pointless An effective way to boost your business is to keep tabs on your sales, your expenditure and your relationships with customers and other businesses. All this information can be stored and compiled into reports for you to analyse and find ways to boost your productivity and a multitude of other uses like capitalising on new opportunities and streamlining expenditure.

The risks that might impact digital projects

Digital projects can fail for a number of reasons. Such as miscommunication. You can have the most talented team in the world but if the team does not communicate effectively then you project will likely fail. This is something that needs to be kept on top off to make sure everyone is on the same page at all times. Some projects are ill conceived. They are either too ambitious, or simply not detailed enough to meet a reasonable end goal. 1/5th of all projects fail right out the gate due to the project being considered a waste of time. Another way projects fail. Is that that aren’t tested out the intended user. Having a project that solves the problem is great but if its difficult to use or adapt to for the actual user then its not going to be used effectively or maybe even at all. And if after months of training, your project is still difficult to use. It will just be abandoned. A large reason for failure is underestimation of what is needed for the project to be done and how longs these things may take. This can either be undershooting the time frame for making a particular part. Which can cause major set backs and really demotivate a team. Which then causes further problems later in the project. Or it can be underestimating how hard a project will be. This can grind the process to a halt and really has the danger off destroying a projects chances of success.

How GDPR impacts digital projects

GDPR is outlined in 8 basic rules. The right to access, this means the individual has the right to access their personal data and how their date is used by the company if requested. The right ro be forgotten, if the consumer are no longer customers of the company or if the request their data to be deleted, then they have the right for it to be so. The right to data portability, meaning the transfer of their data to another service provider in a commonly used and readable machine format. The right to be informed, which covers any gathering of their data by companies, the individual must be informed before this happens and consent must first be given. The right to have information corrected, making sure that all data held by the company is accurate and up to date. The right to restrict processing, requesting that your data is held, but not used. The right to object, this gives the right of the individual to stop the processing of their data for direct marketing, it must happen as soon as the request in received without exception. And finally the right to be notified, this means if there has been a data breach which has compromised your personal data then you have the right to be informed within 72 hours of the company becoming aware of the breach. If GDPR is not followed correctly, very heavy fines of up to 20 million euros or 4% of their annual global revenue.
